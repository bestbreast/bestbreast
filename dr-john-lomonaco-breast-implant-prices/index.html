Breast implants do not cause joint pain

Breast implants, saline or silicone, do not cause joint pains. Period. The FDA has established that the implants are innocuous. Your arthritis comes from something else. Check with your doctor about possible causes.

Implants

Augmentation and athletes

A sub pectoral pectoral implant will affect the pectoralis major muscle. The muscle has to be weakened in order to place the implant under the muscle. When the muscle is activated the implant will be displaced.

A professional athlete probably shouldn’t do a sub pectoral implant.

Do I Need a Breast Lift with My Breast Augmentation?

Based upon the photos you have provided, you will require a breast lift. If you undergo breast augmentation without having a lift, you will not be pleased with the appearance.

The breast lift will leave scars around your areola, and a vertical scar that extends down from you areola, but with time these scars fade considerably.

Definitely meet with a board certified plastic surgeon to find out more.

Saline breast implant explantation

Typically it is a relatively simple procedure to remove a saline implant especially when the capsule is left intact. If a capsulectomy is also performed, this may cause a bit more bleeding and some surgeons will use a drain for a day or two.

It is best to wait at least 3 months post breastfeeding

Dr John Lomonaco Before And After Photos (1)

I would recommend waiting at least 3 months since your last breast feeding before proceeding with breast augmentation surgery. It does not hurt to wait longer if you would like to be sure that you are no longer lactating.

It is important to make sure you are no longer lactating prior to surgery to allow your breasts to shrink down to their prepregnancy size.

Waiting at least 3 months will also decrease the amount of vascularity in your breasts and decrease any bleeding you may encounter with surgery.

Another potential complication you can develop while still lactating is development of a galactocoel. This is a collection of milk that can accumulate within the breast pocket which can possibly become infected causing your implant to need to be removed.

If you are still expressing any milk, I would wait until this stops before proceeding with surgery. This will decrease your chance of developing any complications. I hope you find this information helpful.

Consider your breast footprint and body shape

It sounds like your surgeon is giving you good advice. Consider the base width or footprint of your breast. As you go beyond that dimension of your breast you risk injuring nerves critical for sensation to your nipple.

Furthermore, consider your height and weight, the size of your waist and bottom; and how much curve you want to create in your breasts relative to the rest of your body. Also, beyond a certain implant size the risks of bottoming out, rippling, and thinning your tissues goes up.
