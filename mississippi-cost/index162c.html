Symptomatic Breast Implants should be evaluated

There is no “Expiration Date” on your breast implants, and I have seen many patients with implants in place for 20 years or more. If a patient is symptom free, there is no need to replace the implants in MS. On the other hand, implants are medical devices that may develop problems over time, such as capsular contracture, rupture, infection, rippling, etc.

In addition, women with breast implants must still be vigilant in their screening for breast cancer. Women with breast implants who have pain, changes in the appearance or shape of the breast should be evaluated by a qualified Plastic Surgeon to make sure there is no complication from the device. (Robert J. Carpenter, MD, Cumberland Plastic Surgeon)

Breast implant lifespan

There is definitely a common misconception about the lifespan of breast implants. I have patients come in frequently and say things like “I am supposed to have these changed every ten years, right?”

To make the waters muddier, the FDA encourages women to have their silicone implants imaged with an MRI every 2-3 years even without symptoms. Breast implants are not lifetime devices. As noted below, the rupture rate for saline is a cumulative 1% per year, somewhere around 10-15% at 10 years.

When there is a rupture, the event is very obvious as the implant deflates and the breast is smaller. Silicone gel implants in MS, on the other hand, may rupture without signs or symptoms. The rupture rates with the new implants are in the range of 6-13% at 10 years depending on the implant manufacturer. My general recommendations regarding saline implants are to replace them in the event of rupture or significant capsule contracture (hardness or change in shape). With silicone implants, I inform the patients that the FDA recommends serial MRI scans every 2-3 years.

The woman may or may not pursue an MRI at their expense. If the implants are soft, look nice, and the patient is without symptoms, I see no reason to remove the implants, even at 15-20 years post surgery.

The question arises as how to treat a silent implant rupture detected by an US or MRI. That is a decision that I leave to the patient. If the silicone gel is ruptured within the capsule (scar sac around the implant) but the patient is asymptomatic, then you may be able to observe with periodic examinations. In summary, there are no hard and fast rules with regards to mandatory removal. This is a personal, patient-specific decision based on how the implants look and feel. With regards to your personal questions, it is rare but possible to have a delayed infection, or perhaps this is a heat rash due to the breast hanging over the upper abdominal skin. How to select breast implant sizes so you are happy with the results after

There are so many factors that come into play with breast implant size selection. The first is how much breast tissue you have and then how big you wish to be. The second is how wide is your chest and how much cleavage you wish to have.

Regarding the first question you should measure the size of your chest below your breast in inches to know what bra size you wear. For example if you measure 30″ below your breast you should add 4 inches to that number and you would wear a 34 bra.

Then you measure the fullest part of your breast to know the cup size. If you measure 34″ then you are a 34A, if you measure 35″ then you are a B and so on. The usual rule of thumb is for every 150-200cc you should increase your cup size by one.

So a 400 cc for an 34A cup should easily be a full C. The second is to measure the base width of your breast to know how wide of an implant you can fit. Based upon that information in centimeters you would then select the implant with that width and the volume to match your size.

It is the width of the implant that determines the closeness of the cleavage area. For example if your base width is 13 cm the maximal size in the HP style of silicone breast implant is 500 cc. If you select the MP+ style which is a wider but less projecting implant your maximal size would be 400 cc. The other helpful way to measure volume you desire is to use the Mentor implant sizing system. Here you would try on their special implant sizers fitted under a bra and you can see and feel the size and weight of the implant to gauge how big you wish to be. Finally if your surgeon has the Vectra XT computer imaging system which we have in our office. The program can simulate certain sizes on your own body to help you visualize the after appearance. I hope this helps in your decision regarding breast implant size selection. (Benjamin Chu, MD, FACS, Honolulu Plastic Surgeon)
